Some engineering teams seem to move fast and stay aligned. Others spend half their planning cycles re-litigating old decisions. The difference, almost always, comes down to one thing: whether the team treats decisions as artifacts worth preserving.

A decision culture isn't a process. It's a habit. And like most good habits, it starts small.

## Why It Matters More Than You Think

Engineering decisions are not ephemeral. They shape the codebase, the team structure, the product roadmap — sometimes for years. When they're undocumented, every person who joins the team inherits a black box.

The senior engineer who made the call three years ago understood the tradeoffs. They considered the alternatives. They had context — market timing, team skills, scaling assumptions — that informed the choice. When that context is lost, the decision looks arbitrary. And arbitrary decisions get challenged.

A decision culture means: **the reasoning travels with the decision.** Not in someone's head. In writing.

## What a Decision Culture Looks Like in Practice

You don't need a wiki. You don't need a new tool. You need a lightweight, consistent practice.

**Log decisions where they're made.** Most engineering decisions happen in Slack. That's where the conversation is — and that's where the log should be. If logging a decision requires switching contexts, opening a new tab, filling out a form — it won't happen. The friction kills the habit.

**Capture three things: what, why, and who.** Every decision log should answer: what was decided, why this option over alternatives, and who made the call. That's it. A sentence or two per field is enough.

**Make it searchable.** A decision that's logged but not findable is almost as useless as one that was never logged. When a new engineer asks "why is this service async?" — the answer needs to surface in a search, not require someone to remember the right Slack thread from 14 months ago.

**Make it normal to reference logged decisions.** The habit compounds when people start citing past decisions in planning discussions. "We actually logged this call in Q2 — here's the reasoning." That single moment — when a logged decision saves an hour of debate — is when the culture clicks.

## How to Start

Start with the next consequential decision your team makes. Not everything needs to be logged — but the ones that shape architecture, process, or direction do.

Ask yourself: "If a new engineer joins in six months and asks why we did this, is there a record they can find?" If the answer is no, log it.

Over time, the log becomes a resource. New engineers onboard faster. Planning meetings move faster. The team builds on a foundation of documented reasoning instead of inherited assumptions.

## The Compounding Return

A decision culture doesn't pay off immediately. The first few logs feel like overhead. But at month three, when someone asks "didn't we already decide this?" — and you can pull up the record in five seconds — the value is obvious.

Teams with strong decision cultures don't just move faster. They argue less. They onboard better. They build more consistently. Because they're not starting from scratch every time the context shifts.
