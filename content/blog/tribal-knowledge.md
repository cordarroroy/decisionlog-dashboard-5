Every engineering team has it. That one senior engineer who "just knows" why the codebase is structured the way it is. Why the API is async. Why the team chose DynamoDB over Postgres three years ago. Why that one service runs on a separate infrastructure.

This is tribal knowledge — and it's silently costing your team more than you realize.

## Where Decisions Go to Die

Slack is where engineering teams live. And Slack is where decisions get made every day: architecture choices, technology tradeoffs, process changes, prioritization calls. The conversation happens, the decision lands, and then it disappears into thread history forever.

Nobody means for this to happen. It's just the default. You're in a meeting, a call, a quick DM exchange — and a real decision gets made in 20 messages that no one will ever find again.

The result? Your team is building on an invisible foundation. New engineers have no way to understand *why* things are the way they are. Decisions get re-debated because nobody remembers they were already made. Architecture choices get questioned every quarter.

## The Real Cost

Here's what tribal knowledge actually costs, concretely:

**Onboarding takes months instead of weeks.** A new engineer joins your team and spends their first six weeks asking questions that should have answers somewhere. "Why did we go with this authentication approach?" "Why is this service split out?" The answers exist — in someone's memory, or in a Slack thread from 18 months ago that nobody will find.

**Rework happens on a predictable schedule.** Every quarter, your planning meeting re-opens a decision that was made last quarter. You spend an hour re-debating Postgres vs. MongoDB, or monolith vs. microservices. Not because circumstances changed — but because nobody wrote it down.

**Knowledge leaves when people leave.** When your most senior engineer goes on leave or changes jobs, they take institutional knowledge with them. The decisions they made, the context behind them, the tradeoffs they considered — gone. Your team inherits a codebase with no manual.

## The Fix Is Simpler Than You Think

The solution isn't a new tool. It's a new habit — and it only needs to happen where your team already works: Slack.

When a decision gets made in a Slack thread, log it. Capture the context, the reasoning, the alternatives considered. Make it searchable. That's it.

The next time someone asks "why did we do it this way?" — there's an answer. Not in someone's memory. Not in a thread from two years ago. In a searchable log that anyone on the team can access in seconds.

**The teams that document decisions don't just onboard faster. They build better products.** Because they're not starting from scratch every quarter. They're building on a foundation of institutional memory.
